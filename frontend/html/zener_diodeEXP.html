Theory
Background concepts & working principle

A Zener diode is a specially fabricated PN junction diode designed to operate reliably in the reverse breakdown region. Unlike ordinary diodes that can be permanently damaged by reverse breakdown, a Zener diode is engineered to sustain reverse current without damage, making it ideal for voltage regulation.

Key Principle: In forward bias, a Zener diode behaves identically to a normal PN junction diode — conducting once the threshold (~0.6–0.7 V for silicon) is exceeded. In reverse bias, it blocks current until the applied voltage reaches the Zener voltage (Vz), after which it conducts heavily while maintaining a nearly constant voltage across its terminals.

Forward Bias: When the anode is connected to the positive terminal and the cathode to the negative terminal, the depletion region narrows. Current begins to flow significantly once the forward voltage crosses the cut-in voltage (~0.3 V for germanium, ~0.7 V for silicon). Beyond this, current increases exponentially with voltage.

Reverse Bias: When the polarity is reversed, the depletion region widens and only a very small leakage current flows — until the reverse voltage reaches the Zener breakdown voltage (Vz). At this point, two phenomena contribute to breakdown:

Zener Effect (high doping, low Vz < 5V)
Avalanche Effect (lower doping, Vz > 5V)
Voltage remains ≈ Vz after breakdown

Procedure
Step-by-step experimental instructions

The experiment is performed in two parts. Follow both procedures carefully and record readings systematically in the Observations section.

☀️ Forward Bias
  1. Connect the Zener diode in forward bias — anode to positive, cathode to negative terminal of the supply.
  2. Set the supply voltage to zero initially. Switch on the power supply.
  3. Gradually increase the voltage in small steps using the potentiometer.
  4. At each step, record the forward voltage (Vf) from the voltmeter and forward current (If) from the milliammeter.
  5. Continue until the current rises steeply (typically beyond 0.5–0.7 V). Do not exceed safe current limits.
  6. Plot the Vf vs If graph to obtain the forward characteristic curve.
🌙 Reverse Bias
  1. Reconnect the circuit in reverse bias — cathode to positive, anode to negative terminal.
  2. Set voltage to zero. Switch on the supply.
  3. Slowly increase the reverse voltage in small increments.
  4. Observe and record reverse current (Ir) — it remains very small initially.
  5. Note the voltage at which current increases sharply — this is the Zener breakdown voltage (Vz).
  6. Record additional readings in the breakdown region. Plot Vr vs Ir to determine Vz from the graph.
Tip: Take readings at very fine voltage intervals (0.1 V steps) near the cut-in region (forward) and near the breakdown region (reverse) to plot an accurate, smooth characteristic curve.

Precautions
Safety and accuracy guidelines

Strictly follow these precautions to ensure accurate results and avoid damage to components:

  • 1 Always verify the polarity of the Zener diode before connecting it to the circuit — anode and cathode must be correctly identified (use the diode's marking or a multimeter in diode-test mode).
  • 2 Increase the supply voltage gradually in small steps, especially near the cut-in region (forward bias) and near the expected breakdown voltage (reverse bias). Sudden large increases can damage the diode.
  • 3 Do not exceed the rated breakdown voltage or rated maximum current of the Zener diode, as this can cause permanent damage or destruction of the component.
  • 4 Always include a current-limiting resistor in series with the Zener diode to prevent excessive current flow in the breakdown region.
  • 5 Use proper meter ranges — start with the highest range for both the voltmeter and milliammeter and progressively switch to lower ranges for more accurate readings.
  • 6 Ensure all connections are tight and secure before switching on the power supply. Loose or wrong connections can lead to incorrect readings or short circuits.
  • 7 Take readings only after the readings stabilize — wait a few seconds after changing the voltage before recording milliammeter and voltmeter values.

Viva Q&A
Click a question to reveal the answer

Prepare for your lab viva using these commonly asked questions on Zener diode characteristics. Click any question to expand its answer.

1 What is a Zener diode?

A Zener diode is a heavily doped PN junction diode specially designed to operate in the reverse breakdown region without being destroyed. It maintains a nearly constant voltage (the Zener voltage, Vz) across its terminals once the reverse breakdown voltage is reached. It is widely used as a voltage regulator and reference element in electronic circuits.

2 What is breakdown voltage in a Zener diode?

The breakdown voltage (Vz) is the specific reverse voltage at which the Zener diode begins to conduct heavily in reverse bias. At this voltage, the electric field across the depletion region is strong enough to initiate either the Zener effect (quantum tunneling, for Vz < 5 V) or the avalanche effect (impact ionization, for Vz > 5 V). This voltage is stable and used as a reference. In our experiment, Vz ≈ 8 V.

3 Why is the Zener diode used in circuits?

The Zener diode is used primarily because it maintains a constant voltage across its terminals when operating in the breakdown region, regardless of variations in input voltage or load current (within limits). This property makes it invaluable for: voltage regulation (stabilizing supply voltages), voltage reference in precision circuits, clipping/clamping in waveform shaping, and overvoltage protection for sensitive components.

4 In which bias condition does the Zener diode operate for regulation?

For voltage regulation purposes, the Zener diode operates in reverse bias. When connected in reverse and with the supply voltage exceeding Vz, the diode enters the breakdown region and clamps the voltage at Vz. In forward bias, it behaves like an ordinary diode and cannot provide regulation; its forward voltage drop (~0.7 V for silicon) is small and not useful for regulation. The unique reverse-bias breakdown property is what distinguishes Zener diodes from standard rectifier diodes.

5 What are the practical applications of a Zener diode?

Zener diodes find wide practical use across electronics and power systems:

1. Voltage Regulators — Maintains constant output voltage in power supplies.
2. Voltage Reference — Provides precise reference voltage in op-amp circuits and ADCs.
3. Overvoltage Protection — Protects ICs and sensitive components from voltage spikes.
4. Waveform Clipping — Clips signal peaks in analog signal conditioning.
5. Level Shifting — Shifts signal levels between different circuit stages.
6. Surge Protection — Used in transient voltage suppressors (TVS diodes) to absorb surges.

6 What is the difference between Zener effect and Avalanche effect?

Zener Effect: Occurs in heavily doped junctions with a thin depletion region and Vz < 5 V. The strong electric field directly breaks covalent bonds through quantum tunneling, generating electron-hole pairs. Temperature coefficient is negative (Vz decreases with temperature).

Avalanche Effect: Occurs in lightly doped junctions with Vz > 5 V. High-energy carriers accelerated by the electric field collide with atoms and knock out more carriers — a multiplicative chain reaction (impact ionization). Temperature coefficient is positive (Vz increases with temperature).

In practice, diodes near 5 V have both effects, giving near-zero temperature coefficient — ideal for stable voltage references.
