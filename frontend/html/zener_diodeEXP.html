Theory
Background concepts & working principle

A Zener diode is a specially fabricated PN junction diode designed to operate reliably in the reverse breakdown region. Unlike ordinary diodes that can be permanently damaged by reverse breakdown, a Zener diode is engineered to sustain reverse current without damage, making it ideal for voltage regulation.

Key Principle: In forward bias, a Zener diode behaves identically to a normal PN junction diode — conducting once the threshold (~0.6–0.7 V for silicon) is exceeded. In reverse bias, it blocks current until the applied voltage reaches the Zener voltage (Vz), after which it conducts heavily while maintaining a nearly constant voltage across its terminals.

Forward Bias: When the anode is connected to the positive terminal and the cathode to the negative terminal, the depletion region narrows. Current begins to flow significantly once the forward voltage crosses the cut-in voltage (~0.3 V for germanium, ~0.7 V for silicon). Beyond this, current increases exponentially with voltage.

Reverse Bias: When the polarity is reversed, the depletion region widens and only a very small leakage current flows — until the reverse voltage reaches the Zener breakdown voltage (Vz). At this point, two phenomena contribute to breakdown:

Zener Effect (high doping, low Vz < 5V)
Avalanche Effect (lower doping, Vz > 5V)
Voltage remains ≈ Vz after breakdown
Used for voltage reference & regulation

Procedure
Step-by-step experimental instructions

The experiment is performed in two parts. Follow both procedures carefully and record readings systematically in the Observations section.

☀️ Forward Bias
  1. Connect the Zener diode in forward bias — anode to positive, cathode to negative terminal of the supply.
  2. Set the supply voltage to zero initially. Switch on the power supply.
  3. Gradually increase the voltage in small steps using the potentiometer.
  4. At each step, record the forward voltage (Vf) from the voltmeter and forward current (If) from the milliammeter.
  5. Continue until the current rises steeply (typically beyond 0.5–0.7 V). Do not exceed safe current limits.
  6. Plot the Vf vs If graph to obtain the forward characteristic curve.
🌙 Reverse Bias
  1. Reconnect the circuit in reverse bias — cathode to positive, anode to negative terminal.
  2. Set voltage to zero. Switch on the supply.
  3. Slowly increase the reverse voltage in small increments.
  4. Observe and record reverse current (Ir) — it remains very small initially.
  5. Note the voltage at which current increases sharply — this is the Zener breakdown voltage (Vz).
  6. Record additional readings in the breakdown region. Plot Vr vs Ir to determine Vz from the graph.
Tip: Take readings at very fine voltage intervals (0.1 V steps) near the cut-in region (forward) and near the breakdown region (reverse) to plot an accurate, smooth characteristic curve.

⚡ What is Zener Breakdown? — Theory & Concepts
▼
🔬
Zener Breakdown
When reverse voltage across the diode reaches the breakdown voltage (V_Z), the electric field becomes strong enough to pull electrons from their covalent bonds — creating a sharp, controlled current surge. Unlike regular diodes, Zener diodes are designed to operate safely in this region.
V_Z = 6.2 V (at I_Z = 5 mA)
📐
Voltage Regulation
The key property: once breakdown occurs, the voltage across the Zener stays approximately constant at V_Z, regardless of the current through it. Any additional supply voltage drops across the series resistor R_S. This makes Zener diodes ideal for reference voltage and regulation circuits.
V_R = V_supply − V_Z
I_Z = V_R / R_S
📈
V-I Regions
The V-I curve has three key regions in reverse bias: (1) Leakage Region — tiny reverse current below V_Z; (2) Breakdown Knee — rapid transition at V_Z ≈ 6.2V; (3) Regulation Region — steep near-vertical curve where voltage is clamped. In forward bias, the diode conducts like a normal silicon diode above ~0.65V.
I = I_S · (e^(V/nV_T) − 1)
⚠️
Precautions
Safety and accuracy guidelines

Strictly follow these precautions to ensure accurate results and avoid damage to components:

  • 1Always verify the polarity of the Zener diode before connecting it to the circuit.
  • 2Increase the supply voltage gradually in small steps, especially near the cut-in and breakdown regions.
  • 3Do not exceed the rated breakdown voltage or rated maximum current of the Zener diode.
  • 4Always include a current-limiting resistor in series with the Zener diode.
  • 5Use proper meter ranges — start with the highest range and progressively switch to lower ranges.
  • 6Ensure all connections are tight and secure before switching on the power supply.
  • 7Take readings only after the readings stabilize — wait a few seconds after changing voltage.

Viva Q&A
Click a question to reveal the answer

Prepare for your lab viva using these commonly asked questions on Zener diode characteristics.

1What is a Zener diode?

A Zener diode is a heavily doped PN junction diode specially designed to operate in the reverse breakdown region without being destroyed. It maintains a nearly constant voltage (Vz) across its terminals once the reverse breakdown voltage is reached.

2What is breakdown voltage in a Zener diode?

The breakdown voltage (Vz) is the specific reverse voltage at which the Zener diode begins to conduct heavily. At this voltage, either the Zener effect (quantum tunneling, Vz < 5V) or avalanche effect (impact ionization, Vz > 5V) initiates stable, controlled conduction.

3Why is the Zener diode used in circuits?

The Zener diode maintains a constant voltage across its terminals in the breakdown region. This makes it valuable for: voltage regulation, voltage reference in precision circuits, clipping/clamping in waveform shaping, and overvoltage protection for sensitive components.

4In which bias condition does the Zener diode operate for regulation?

For voltage regulation, the Zener diode operates in reverse bias. When the supply voltage exceeds Vz, the diode clamps the output at Vz. In forward bias, it behaves like an ordinary diode and cannot provide voltage regulation.

5What is the difference between Zener effect and Avalanche effect?

Zener Effect: Occurs in heavily doped junctions (Vz < 5V) via quantum tunneling. Negative temperature coefficient.

Avalanche Effect: Occurs in lightly doped junctions (Vz > 5V) via impact ionization chain reactions. Positive temperature coefficient. Diodes near 5V combine both effects, giving near-zero temperature coefficient — ideal for stable voltage references.

Pro Tip: During the viva, always relate the Zener diode's behaviour back to the characteristic curve. Mention the three regions — forward conduction, reverse blocking, and Zener breakdown — clearly by name.
