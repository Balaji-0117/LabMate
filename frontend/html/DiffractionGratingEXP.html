Aim
Objective of this experiment

To determine the wavelength of monochromatic laser light using a diffraction grating by analysing interference patterns and verifying the grating equation nλ = d sin θ.

Learning Objective: Develop an intuitive understanding of how a diffraction grating splits monochromatic laser light into discrete orders of maxima. Students will observe the spatial distribution of bright spots, measure their positions, and use the grating equation to compute the wavelength with precision.

By the end of this experiment, you will be able to trace the formation of diffraction orders, apply the grating equation to calculate wavelength, measure angular positions of maxima, and understand how grating spacing and order number affect the diffraction pattern.

Description
Theory & background
1

Anatomy of a Diffraction Grating

At its core, a grating is a collection of thousands of equally spaced parallel slits. When a laser beam strikes these slits, each one acts as a new point source of waves, causing them to spread outward (Huygens' Principle).

2

The Principle of Interference

As these secondary waves travel, they overlap. Where two waves meet peak-to-peak they reinforce (Constructive Interference), creating a bright spot. Where peak meets trough, they cancel (Destructive Interference). Bright maxima appear only where the path difference is an exact multiple of the wavelength.

💡 Key Term

The spacing between adjacent slits is the Grating Constant (d). It equals d = 1/N, where N is the number of lines per millimetre on the grating.

3

The Grating Equation

The relationship between wavelength, slit spacing and diffraction angle is captured by a single, elegant formula used to compute the wavelength from measurable quantities.

nλ = d sin θ

Procedure
Step-by-step instructions

Follow each step carefully to measure the diffraction pattern and compute the wavelength.

  1. Place the laser source at the 0 cm mark and the projection screen at 200 cm on the optical bench.
  2. Mount the diffraction grating vertically in the beam path between the laser and screen.
  3. Switch on the laser; a central bright spot (n = 0) and symmetric side spots (n = ±1, ±2…) will appear on the screen.
  4. Record the grating-to-screen distance D carefully from the bench scale.
  5. Measure the distance x from the central maximum to the first-order bright spot on both the left and right sides.
  6. Calculate the mean of both sides: x̄ = (xL + xR) / 2.
  7. Compute sin θ = x̄ / √(x̄² + D²) and then the wavelength: λ = d sin θ / n.
  8. Repeat for different grating positions to get at least 3 readings. Average the results for the final wavelength.
  9. Record all observations in the table under the Simulation tab.
⚠ Safety Note: Never look directly into the laser beam or its first-order reflections. Always direct the laser away from people's eyes and use the beam stop when measurements are not being taken.

Viva Questions
Important viva questions for Diffraction Grating experiment
1
What is a diffraction grating?

A diffraction grating is an optical device with a large number of equally spaced parallel slits (or rulings) that diffracts incident light into its component wavelengths. A typical grating has 600 lines per millimetre, giving a grating constant of d = 1/600 mm ≈ 1.67 × 10⁻⁶ m.

2
State the grating equation and define each term.

The grating equation is nλ = d sin θ, where n is the order of diffraction (integer), λ is the wavelength of incident light, d is the grating constant (slit spacing), and θ is the angle of diffraction from the central maximum.

3
Why is laser light preferred in this experiment?

Laser light is monochromatic (single wavelength), highly coherent, and collimated. These properties produce sharp, well-defined diffraction maxima that are easy to locate and measure accurately, unlike ordinary white light which would produce overlapping, spread-out spectra.

4
What is meant by the order of diffraction?

The order of diffraction (n) indicates the number of whole wavelengths of path difference between adjacent slits. n = 0 is the central undiffracted beam; n = ±1, ±2… are the first, second… order maxima on either side. Higher-order spots are dimmer and further from centre.

5
How does increasing grating spacing (d) affect the diffraction pattern?

From nλ = d sin θ, increasing d (fewer lines per mm) decreases sin θ, meaning the diffraction spots move closer to the central maximum. Conversely, a finer grating (smaller d, more lines/mm) spreads the pattern wider, allowing better angular resolution between orders.

6
What are the main sources of error in this experiment?

Key sources of error include: (1) grating not perpendicular to the incident beam, (2) imprecise measurement of the spot position x or grating-to-screen distance D, (3) parallax error while reading the scale, and (4) vibration of the optical bench causing spot drift. Taking multiple readings and averaging reduces random error.
